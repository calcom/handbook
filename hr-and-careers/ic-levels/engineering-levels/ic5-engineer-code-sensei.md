# 🥷 IC5 Engineer (Code Sensei)

* **Role**: IC5s (Staff Engineers) operate as force multipliers across the engineering org. They are no longer measured primarily by the code they personally write, but by the leverage they create through architecture, tooling, standards, AI workflows, and the engineers they grow. IC5s own technical outcomes that span multiple teams and multiple quarters.
* **Responsibilities**:
  * Partner **directly with the Head of Engineering** to drive major architectural improvements, large-scale refactors, and multi-quarter technical initiatives. You are the go-to collaborator when we need to replatform, untangle, or re-architect a core part of the product.
  * Lead technical initiatives that span multiple teams or surfaces of the product. These are the kind of work that can't be owned by a single IC4. Write the design doc, build consensus, and see it through to production.
  * **Own our AI-assisted development practice.** Define how the team uses tools like Claude Code, Cursor, and Codex effectively. Write the guides, build the prompt libraries, curate the `CLAUDE.md` and `AGENTS.md` files, and lift the floor on what "good AI-assisted output" looks like at Cal.com.
  * **Design the evals and guardrails** for code generated or modified by AI. Make sure AI-assisted PRs hit the same quality bar as human-written ones, via tests, type safety, review checklists, and automated checks.
  * Identify places where AI can ship real product value (agents, assistants, workflow automation inside the app) and lead the technical design for those features end-to-end.
  * Build internal tooling and automations that make the entire engineering team faster: eval harnesses, codegen pipelines, one-off scripts that save hours, internal dashboards, dev-loop improvements.
  * Act as a technical interviewer and a strong voice in hiring. You help set the bar for what an engineer at Cal.com looks like.
  * Mentor IC3s and IC4s. Helping them grow into staff-track engineers, expand their scope, and take on more ambitious technical leadership is part of your job, not a side project.
  * Own response for critical, cross-cutting incidents. When something is on fire across multiple systems, you are one of the people driving it to resolution.
* **Leadership**:
  * IC5s are the engineers other engineers look to when they're stuck on the hard problems: architectural trade-offs, performance puzzles, gnarly edge cases in timezone or scheduling logic, tricky migrations.
  * You are an amplifier. A good week for an IC5 is one where five other engineers shipped better work because of something you built, wrote, reviewed, or explained.
  * You communicate clearly in writing. Design docs, RFCs, post-mortems, and long-form Threads posts are part of the job, not chores to avoid. You make complex decisions understandable to the whole team.
  * When the team is debating tech direction, you bring the discussion to a decision. You don't let important architectural questions drift.
* **Expertise**:
  * Deep expertise in at least one foundational area of Cal.com (scheduling engine, billing, bookings, calendar integrations, availability, workflows, embeds, etc.) and working fluency across the rest of the stack.
  * Elite at TypeScript, Prisma, Next.js, and our testing stack. You know the sharp edges of our codebase and you actively file them down.
  * Modern AI fluency: you can pick the right tool for the job (autonomous agent vs. chat vs. inline edit), you know when AI will save time and when it will cost time, and you can read an AI-generated diff critically.
  * You write code that's easy to delete, easy to extend, and easy for an AI tool to work inside of six months from now.
  * You understand systems, not just features: data flow, consistency, latency, failure modes, and what it costs to operate something in production.
* **How IC5 differs from IC6**: As a small team, basically everything either of you does is felt across the whole team, so the distinction is not about reach. It's about horizon, posture, and the kind of decision being made.
  * IC5 runs the practice; IC6 sets the strategy. You write the prompt libraries the team uses. An IC6 decides whether we're a Claude Code shop, a Cursor shop, or both, and why.
  * You shape what we're shipping this quarter and next. An IC6 shapes where the codebase, the stack, and the engineering practice are heading 18-24 months out.
  * You're a strong voice in architecture debates. An IC6 is often the deciding vote in those rooms because the team has learned to trust their judgment.
  * IC5 is the most senior level where you're still measured primarily by engineering outcomes. IC6 is measured by the long-term technical trajectory of the company.

### Path to IC6 Engineer

**Typical timeframe**: 2-4 years

You need to consistently demonstrate skills of an IC6 in order to be promoted.

* **Shift from tactical leadership to strategic leadership**. Move from driving multi-quarter technical initiatives to defining where the codebase, stack, and engineering practice are heading 18-24 months out. Your job becomes setting technical direction, not just executing on it.
  * _Example_: Drive the migration of a core service onto a new architecture, or define and roll out the company's AI development strategy end-to-end.
* **Set technical vision, not just technical direction**. IC5s decide how we build the next thing. IC6s decide what the next thing is and where the codebase is going 12-24 months out. Start thinking and writing at that horizon.
* **Grow other leaders**. Produce IC4s and IC5s. If engineers you've mentored are getting promoted, that's a leading indicator. If you're the only senior voice on a project, that's a lagging one.
